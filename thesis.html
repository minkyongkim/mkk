OVERVIEW

TITLE

Communicating with Reactions: The role of self-presentational concerns in disclosing emotional reactions via Reactions on Facebook

DESCRIPTION

A mixed-methods research study on the self-presentational concerns of Facebook users in using Reactions

ROLE

Independent undergraduate researcher

METHODS

Survey design, qualitative research, including coding/analysis, statistical analysis, write-up

TOOLS

JMP, R, Qualtrics, Excel

Motivation

Many social media platforms afford lightweight interactions, or one-click tools that enable users to 1) communicate with others, and more broadly 2) visibly show interest towards content.

One such example is Facebook's 'like' button:

On Facebook, roughly 1/2 of users "like" their friends' content at least once a day (Scissors et al, 2016). With Reactions users can be express their feelings with even more granularity towards content. According to existing literature, however, users are cautious about emotional disclosure, especially on SNS, as it exposes what is considered a much "private" and self-revealing process. As a result, users may shy away from being more expressive, or choose to share emotions more selectively.

How might we then better support this experience?

Background

Formulating Hypotheses

What we know from existing literature

  • Sharing and talking about emotional events in face-to-face contexts is a private, interpersonal process. Valence matters - negative emotions are often tied to sensitive information.
  • Because users have less accurate ideas of who can actually see their activity in online contexts, they decide how they share their emotions and hold varying degrees of self-presentational concerns (i.e. anxiety about their online image).
  • Two key factors play a role in this decision-making process: network density and who the sharing is directed towards.
  • Users generally share more emotions, with less negative than positive in dense networks or a network with mostly strong ties
  • If the sharing is directed toward a specific person (e.g. via private messaging) rather than a large group of people, users express more. In private messaging, the conversation in which sharing occurs is also more likely to be about the other person and therefore less self-disclosing.

Based on this information, I hypothesized the following for Reactions:

Valence Frequency
Although Reactions are not particularly descriptive, they publicly disclose to the network. As such, users express positive Reactions more frequently than negative. Valence and Perceived Relational Closeness
Reactions can be used to respond to content posted by those inside or outside the users' network. Negative reactions will be expressed more often for friends' content because the perceived relational closeness is greater. Valence and Self-Presentational Concerns
Observed self-presentational concerns will be higher for negative than for positive Reactions. Self-presentational Concerns and Relational Closeness When reacting to friends' content, self-presentational concerns will diminish. Self-presentational Concerns & Relational Closeness across Valence
Even in situations where users react to friends' content, emotional valence will have an effect on self-presentational concerns, as all behavior is still public. Valence and Post Content
Different Reactions are also used to respond to different types of content, i.e. what the post is about.

Methods

The Study

Study participants

Eligibility criteria: Facebook users (18 y/o and above) who both use Facebook and the Reactions feature at least once a week
Sample size: n = 62

Procedure

Eligible participants completed a survey via Qualtrics consisting of two sections. A survey was chosen because the goal was to have participants think about a past activity and answer several self-reported scale questions.

The first aimed to measure how frequently participants used each of the five emotions (love, haha, sad, angry, wow) in Reactions. They were instructed to log into their Facebook and view their Activity Log to search for the 10 most recent instances of using a one-click interaction (e.g. "Like"/"Reactions") to respond to content. All participants were compensated $15 for their participation in the study.

The second section aimed to capture how the participants used Reactions for each of the five different Reaction emotions. They were asked to report two instances of using each of the five, totaling 10 instances. For each, they briefly described the content in their own words and copy/pasted content, attaching photos/videos if any. They also indicated whether the content poster was a friend/mutual friend/not a friend.

Following these contextual questions, participants answered questions about perceived relational closeness with the content poster and level of online self-consciousness, both measured on a Likert scale.

Data Analysis

Results from the first section of the survey were analyzed for descriptive statistics. Data from the second were analyzed for statistically significant effects of relational closeness and self-presentational concerns using mixed model regression, with participant modeled as the random effect.

To answer whether participants use different Reactions based on content, a one-level, four-category coding scheme was developed to capture contextual information. Two coders coded a random sample (n=51) chosen from an entire data set of 476 instances until agreement was reached (Cohen's kappa = 0.84).

Findings

Data Preparation

Prior to data analyses, each Reaction type was evaluated for its degree of sentiment (positive/neutral/negative) using Stanford NLP's sentiment analysis model. Such characterization by valence is a common way of differentiating different emotion types in emotion literature. It also helped establish a consistent framework for later analyses and discussions.

The model has five classes of sentiment classification: very negative, negative, neutral, positive, and very positive. Classification of each emotional word is expressed in terms of posterior probability; each Reaction was classified based on the category with highest corresponding probability.



Positive vs. Negative Reactions

Position Reactions are used more frequently than negative Reactions. 'Love' accounted for 50% of total Reactions uses in contrast to 6.7% for 'angry'. However, 'sad' was used a bit more frequently than 'wow'.


Self-Presentational Concerns & Relational Closeness across Reactions

Regression analyses showed that relational closeness and Reactions valence only have a marginally significant relationship. Negative Reactions are therefore not necessarily used more with strong ties compared to positive ones. More intense emotions, however, such as 'angry' and 'love' are used more with closer ties than other Reactions.

In addition, users are self-aware when they use Reactions. Interestingly, they are more concerned with their online image when they react with positive Reactions such as 'love' in comparison to neutral ones, e.g. 'haha' or 'wow' and negative Reactions.



Lastly, although self-presentational concerns are greater when perceived relational closeness is lower, there is no interaction effect. Reacting with 'Love' to a friend's content as opposed to a more relationally distant person did not change levels of self-presentational concerns.


Contexts in using different Reactions

Post content was described according to four different categories - entertainment, information/discovery, self-identification, and bonding. Categories are defined as below.

Negative Reactions ('angry', 'sad') were mostly used for entertainment, information/discovery, and bonding. In other words, they were used sparsely for self-identifying content.

Positive Reactions ('love', 'wow') were mostly used to respond to content by network members. 'Haha' was used primarily for entertaining posts.


Design Implications

Unlike 'Likes', Reactions may be appropriated more faithfully in response to the emotions elicited by the content, as shown by the liberal usage of negative Reactions. Self-presentational concerns for each Reaction also did not differ depending on relational closeness.

A key insight is that self-presentational concerns are prevalent when using the feature and that they vary depending on emotional valence. People seem to be concerned with how showing interest in one piece of individual content will impact their online image.

These observations suggest that one-click tools such as Reactions can potentially be designed in ways to help support self-presentational concerns. This is important because Reactions can often be used between friends. If self-presentational concerns undermine usage, this could affect interpersonal relationships online. As such, Reactions, for example, might be designed so that they are less visible to the network or only seen by certain groups within a network.

Limitations & Next Steps

This study highlights how Reactions should be included in the larger discourse of online self-presentation, but does not explore what aspect of self-presentation is particularly relevant. This could be further probed through qualitative research. In addition, the types of content that are likely to stimulate usage are explored, but motivations behind using Reactions still remain unclear. In particular, what motivates people to react to content posted by others outside their network?

Takeaways

Do not overlook pilot testing

Pilot testing can help improve the quality of the survey before officially administering the study. It is crucial that changes not be made mid-study as it produces inconsistent data.

What users report in retrospect may be different from how they act in the moment

Participant reports of self-presentational concerns involved for each instance of using Reactions might not necessarily be accurate because they were asked to recall a prior experience. Not only is recalling difficult, recalling a feeling is even more of an abstract and challenging task.

Self-reported ratings only tell part of the story

Although scale ratings allowed for a test of statistical significance in the relationship between self-presentation and Reaction type, it did not reveal why participants were more or less concerned with their self-presentation for each activity. Perhaps allowing them to explain their reason for choosing a number would help better understand their perceptions and attitudes about self-presentation on Facebook in more depth.
