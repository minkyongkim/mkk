MOTIVATION

Gift-giving is a part of everyone's lives. Whether it is for birthdays, holidays, anniversies, or any other occasion, a gift is always part of the picture.

However, most people struggle when choosing and searching a gift for the receiver, even though they know the receiver well.

How can we facilitate this process for gift-givers?

RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

In order to better understand the context surrounding the gift-giving process, we conducted a contextual inquiry on a student who was interested in searching and buying a gift for Mother's Day.


The contextual inquiry revealed several pain points of gift-givers, such as:

  • Not knowing what to buy without directly asking the recipient
  • Not being able to search for product alternatives efficiently
  • Lack of information about ongoing discount and sales when online shopping


Having conducted our CI on a college student, we decided that it was necessary to refine and set our target user group as college student gift-givers (17-23 year olds).

USABILITY TESTING

To test our prototype for usability, we devised a usability plan consisting of tasks and instructions.

User performance was also evaluated for task completion, time, and errors. After testing, we measured satisfaction levels using 10-point scale items and open-ended questions (e.g. What would you change about the app?)

Usability metrics such as completion success/time per task and number of errors made per task were recorded.

Task Instructions:

The following instruction set was given in three separate sets (Set A, B, C), repeated each time for a different recipient - male/female/group:

  • Task 1: Search for a gift for a male/female student/group
  • Task 2: Navigate to the Wish List
  • Task 3: Navigate to your current orders
  • Task 4: Search for a gift for an existing user profile

FINAL PROTOTYPE

Changes for Final Iteration

Based on our findings, we identified four major areas for improvement and implemented changes accordingly:

  1. More feedback from user actions - Actions such as selecting description and interests had no feedback. When users tapped an icon, they were not sure whether it was registered by the system.
    To give them clear feedback, we decided to grey out buttons when clicked.
  2. Added error prevention - Instead of immediately removing items from cart upon tapping the 'x', we added a popup asking the user to confirm their action.
  3. Increased button sizes - Because users had trouble tapping on buttons in the hamburger menu, we increased the size.
  4. Onboarding process added - Meaning of symbols such as the heart or $ on the gift feed was unclear. Instruction bubbles were added to clear confusion.

TAKEAWAYS

Goals of usability testing should always point back to fulfilling user needs

Usability testing sessions (or any other evaluative form of research) should not be conducted to solely assess the ability of users to navigate an interface.


Asking users whether they find it useful or not can help inspire more ideas

It is often easy to be wary of asking users about a usefulness of an app or feature on the spot, as some may feel pressured to give positive feedback to please designers. But they can also often make helpful suggestions on how to improve the way something works and bring a different set of eyes from the users' perspective.
