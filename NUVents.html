NUVents

OVERVIEW

Project for Human-centered Service Design (DSGN 305)

TEAM MEMBERS:

Kaitlyn Young, Minkyong Kim, Pratap Jayaram

ROLE:

Journey mapping, user interviews, personas, stakeholder mapping, prototyping

DURATION:

April - June 2016

THE PROBLEM

Northwestern students often struggle to deal with the daily sources of stress in their lives.

They see current stress relief programming offered by the university (i.e. Counseling And Psychological Services) as a way of dealing with larger events of mental health problems, but don't feel there is a similar outlet for daily stress.

How might we engage the Northwestern community to serve as supportive and active listeners to each other's stresses?

RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

Stakeholders

Before conducting our user research, we mapped out our stakeholders to help us guide our decision-making early in the process.



Formal In-Depth Interviews

We conducted a series of in-depth interviews with 6 Northwestern students, 20-30 minutes each. We also guerrilla interviewed 9 participants, 3-4 minutes each.

Some questions we asked include:

  1. What are the daily stressors in your life?
  2. What mental health services offered to NU students are you aware of?
  3. What works for you in relieving daily stress?
  4. What are the most important aspects of a stress relief activity?

We also asked existing on-campus services about various options and solutions offered for students in the current space. These include:

  1. The Library UX Team, which helps host mental health activities in the library
  2. NUListens, a peer-listening service

SYNTHESIS

Some common key insights from the user interviews were that:

  • Students prefer the accessibility of family/friends over formal services when relieving stress
  • Students generally felt pressured use personal avenues to deal with daily stress
  • Daily sources of stress are mostly centered around student group responsibilities and academic work

    • To help paint a more detailed picture of nuanced step-by-step thought processes and emotions of our target user, we translated these findings into a journey map and personas of our target user.

    Interestingly, our stakeholder interviews with existing services revealed very similar findings:

  • Students often think daily stress is not "bad" enough to reach out for help
  • Time and location of offered resources are key in determining successful engagement.
  • Students do not necessarily seek advice, but to be heard.

    • IDEATION

    We next used our insights to help us identify opportunity areas by framing it in a futuristic, open-ended way, i.e. by generating key "how-might-we" statements.

    How might we...

    1. Encourage students to use small breaks in between their schedules for stress relief?
    2. Help students utilize their family and friend networks to relieve day-to-day stress?
    3. Get students to integrate physical activity and exercise into their daily schedules?

    This allowed us to think expansively, but also provided focus and direction in our idea generation phase. After multiple rounds of ideation, we decided on the concept of building an app which students can use to vent to other anonymous students.
