OVERVIEW

ZALORA Group is a online fashion e-commerce company based in Singapore and with a presence throughout Southeast Asia - in Malaysia, Indonesia, Philippines, Hong Kong, and Taiwan. ZALORA offers both a wide range of international and local brands in addition to its in-house labels for mens and womens' apparel, shoes, and accessories. ZALORA is a part of Global Fashion Group, which operates e-commerce fashion sites in emerging markets.

DESCRIPTION

Conducting usability tests to improve ZALORA's order tracking module by allowing customers to self-serve in the returns tracking process

ROLE

Usability testing, including planning, execution, findings synthesis, and design recommendations

DURATION

August - September 2018

METHODS

Moderated usability testing

TOOLS

Quicktime player (for recording), Excel

Motivation

ZALORA customers often change their minds or make mistakes when submitting return requests for their purchases via the company's Returns and Exchanges (REMO) module on desktop. As a result, ZALORA's customer experience (CX) team receives the most queries about returns tracking, which can be further defined as:

  • tracking returned items
  • refund status inquiries
  • exchange status inquiries

As of July 2017, such inquiries accounted for the following volumes of CX contacts across markets:

  • Hong Kong (HK) - 30%
  • Singapore (SG) - 27%
  • Malaysia (MY) - 21%
  • Taiwan (TW) - 18%
  • Indonesia (ID) - 10%
  • Philippines (PH) - 10%

The CX team found that NPS scores decreased and contact ratios increased particularly because of returns related issues.



By allowing customers to correct such mistakes and assuming an adoption rate of 50%, returns-related customer contacts would decrease, with a potential cost savings of 15,000 EUR/year.

Based on this, our project goal was to allow customers to self-serve in cancelling their returns.

Usability Testing

Objectives

Given the re-designed module, we were interested in the following research questions:

  1. Do users understand how to raise a cancellation request?
  2. Do users understand the messages communicated at each step of the request submission process?
  3. Do users understand the visual feedback regarding their request outcome reflected in the module?

Participants

4 participants, ranging from new to frequent ZALORA shoppers

All were pre-screened for eligibility based on ZALORA's target age group (25-34 y/o), prior study participation experience, and usage of the existing module that enables customers to return items. Participants were compensated SGD $30 for their time.

Procedure

Each usability testing session lasted approximately 60 min. In each session, participants were asked to spend the first 30 minutes with a clickable prototype and perform tasks while thinking out loud. After the test, participants were asked to rate and describe their overall experience in using the prototype on a 7-point bipolar emotional response testing (BERT) scale. We were interested in capturing various aspects of their attitudes and perceptions, as opposed to their thoughts on general factors such as satisfaction, ease of use, etc.

For Task 1, 1a, and 2, the following quantitative metrics were collected to evaluate the usability of the feature in addition to qualitative data:

  1. Task completion success

  2. Task completion time

  3. Number of errors

Only success rate was measured for Task 3.

Recommendations

Task Recommendations
Task 1: Cancelling a return request CTA is clear; no changes needed.
Task 1a: What do you understand from the cancellation process throughout? Reconsider entry points for such messages in emails or app notifications. The copy can also be revised to be more clear. In addition, consider giving in-app or email feedback upon cancellation confirmation.

Most (3/4) skipped reading the message about how return requests cannot be cancelled if items are already dropped off and how to resubmit a cancellation request.
Task 2: Raising a new request Consider creating a separate CTA to differentiate it from the main one and re-position it near the "last updated" text. Also re-organize multiple CTAs to reduce confusion for users.

Most (3/4) did not know to click the "Return Items" CTA to resubmit a cancellation request because the CTA did not reflect any of their most recent actions, i.e. cancelling a return request.
Task 3a: Understanding the status of a successful cancellation The status change needs to reflect that a cancellation request has been attempted and successfully processed, e.g. provide feedback message that reads "cancellation successful"

All participants failed to interpret the "Delivered" status as successful cancellation.
Task 3b: Understanding the status of an unsuccessful cancellation Provide feedback message that reads "cancellation unsuccessful" instead along with an explanation. In addition, provide users with a way out of this failure state, i.e. next steps they can take.

Similar to the case above, all participants failed to interpret "Exchange/Refund Requested" as an unsuccessful cancellation and wanted to know why it was unsuccessful. The current status is not an accurate reflection of the actions taken on the order, as the user had attempted to cancel the refund request.

BERT Results

Average ratings on each attribute show that the overall experience was pretty stressful (vs. calm) for users. They felt that the number of steps involved or information to be processed was overwhelming. Content layout should be adjusted in a more readable manner and amount/length of information reduced.

Takeaways

Clear copywriting is often a critical part of good UX

Copywriting is often dismissed when designing for the users' experience, but is actually essential and should be prioritized. It should align with users' mental models and be as accurate as possible, as system feedback is mostly communicated through words.

Set realistic scenarios, especially for complex tasks

Some complex tasks may be hard for users to wrap their head around and influence their ability to perform tasks, potentially biasing results. Setting up realistic task scenarios and making sure that they understand the hypothetical situation before is key to mitigate these effects.
